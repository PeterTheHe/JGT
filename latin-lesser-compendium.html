Nouns

Nouns can be put into a number of cases:


  1. Nominative: The subject of the main verb.
    • e.g.Caecillius an mater
  2. Vocative: Used to address. Ends with an e in the second delension and an i for nouns ending in ius. Otherwise it ends in the same way as the nominative.
    • e.g. tace Quinte! salve Caecillii!
  3. Accusative: The direct object of the main verb.
    • e.g. Quintus puellam vidit
  4. Genitive: Means 'of'. Usually denotes possession.
    • e.g.vocem feminae
  5. Dative: Means 'to' or 'for'. Can be used as the indirect object of some verbs (e.g. credere). The possessive dative (e.g. 'there is to him a book') is translated as if the verb 'habere' had been used (i.e. 'he has a book').
    • e.g. Quintus donum Caecilio dat an mercatoribus pecuniis sunt
  6. Ablative: Means 'by,' 'with', or 'from'. Can also be used in ablative absolute constructions.
    • e.g. puella, domino territa, nihil dixit
  7. Locative: Means 'in' 'at', 'on' or "by". Only exists for the names of cities, islands and a few other words (e.g. 'domus' which becomes 'domi'). By Classical Antiquity the Locative had become effectively redunant, being replaced with the dative, genitive and ablative.
    • e.g. manebo Romae
